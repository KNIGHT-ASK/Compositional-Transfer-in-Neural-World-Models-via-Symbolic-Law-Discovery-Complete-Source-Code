% ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
We leverage isolated physical environments as deterministic testbeds to study whether neural world models can truly internalize and compose modular physical invariants. While deep networks excel at modeling high-dimensional correlations, robust physical understanding requires representations that obey the linear superposition of vector fields. In this paper, we demonstrate that neural models trained via \emph{residual state prediction} on isolated systems---such as gravity or Hookean elasticity---can be composed zero-shot to predict combined environments never encountered during training. Our results show that this modular composition achieves accuracy comparable to structural baselines like Neural ODEs trained on $100{,}000$ target transitions, while monolithic MLPs face catastrophic out-of-distribution divergence. Furthermore, we extract interpretable symbolic laws via sparse regression, achieving $4.7\times$ higher composition accuracy. To validate scalability, we demonstrate the sufficiency of tangent-space mapping for the superposition of additive vector fields and prove that in highly non-linear $2$D Orbital Mechanics, the zero-shot modular ensemble ($0.11 \times 10^{-4}$ MSE) statistically outperforms $100$K-data monolithic baselines ($0.19 \times 10^{-4}$). These results establish a mathematically rigorous framework for structured world modeling where knowledge is accumulated asynchronously and composed algebraically.\footnote{The complete source code is available at: \url{https://github.com/KNIGHT-ASK/Compositional-Transfer-in-Neural-World-Models-via-Symbolic-Law-Discovery-Complete-Source-Code}}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Learning accurate models of the physical world is a foundational step toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). While large language models (LLMs) elegantly master statistical text correlations, true physical understanding requires internalizing invariant rules that combine deterministically. To study this, we use classical mechanics not merely as a simulation task, but as a mathematically tractable testbed for investigating whether neural networks can genuinely internalize and compose deterministic rules. Current neural world models---such as Dreamer \citep{hafner2020dreamer}, MuZero \citep{schrittwieser2020muzero}, and IRIS \citep{micheli2023iris}---typically learn environment dynamics monolithically. While such models can simulate complex environments by interpolating within their training distribution, their representations are fundamentally entangled. If a physical setting changes---for instance, by introducing a new force or combining previously isolated systems---the model requires retraining.

This entanglement limits zero-shot compositional transfer. In classical mechanics, dynamics are inherently compositional: the total acceleration of an object is the vector sum of its constituent forces. A neural network that truly understands physics should exhibit this same compositional structure, allowing independently learned dynamics to be superposed without environment-specific data gathering.

We present a method for achieving compositional zero-shot transfer across physical environments through three key design decisions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Residual state prediction.} Networks predict the change in state $\Delta \mathbf{s} = \mathbf{s}_{t+1} - \mathbf{s}_t$ rather than the absolute next state, isolating the acceleration signal from the kinematic baseline.
    \item \textbf{Shuffled training.} Training data is shuffled to break temporal correlations, forcing the network to learn state-dependent laws rather than trajectory-dependent patterns.
    \item \textbf{Symbolic extraction.} Sparse regression (SINDy) \citep{brunton2016sindy} applied to trained network outputs discovers interpretable symbolic equations that compose without the hallucination and double-counting artifacts that plague direct neural composition.
\end{enumerate}

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We demonstrate zero-shot compositional transfer between neural world models trained on isolated physical laws, achieving accuracy equivalent to 2{,}000-step direct training.
    \item We demonstrate that the compositional framework applies \emph{hierarchically}: decomposing a single environment into continuous dynamics and discrete contact events recovers exact gravity ($9.80$) and discovers the coefficient of restitution as a new physical constant.
    \item We show that symbolic equations extracted from black-box MLPs via sparse regression compose with 4.7$\times$ higher accuracy than direct neural output summation.
    \item We formally probe the epistemic boundary limits of our framework on non-linear geometries (e.g., $S^1$ pendulums) and out-of-library forces, proving that continuous \texttt{Tanh} priors perfectly recover local Taylor expansions without magnitude collapse.
    \item We provide ablation studies isolating the contributions of residual framing (47.3$\times$ improvement) and shuffled training (8.8$\times$ improvement in invariance).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Neural World Models.}
Learned environment simulators have become central to model-based reinforcement learning. Ha and Schmidhuber's World Models \citep{ha2018worldmodels} use variational autoencoders with recurrent components; Dreamer \citep{hafner2020dreamer} extends this with latent imagination for planning; MuZero \citep{schrittwieser2020muzero} learns dynamics models without access to environment observations. All learn monolithic dynamics—none support compositional transfer across environments.

\paragraph{Physics-Informed Neural Networks.}
PINNs \citep{raissi2019pinns} embed known governing equations (PDEs) into the loss function, providing physics priors that improve generalization. However, PINNs require the governing equations to be \emph{known a priori}, whereas our approach discovers them from data.

\paragraph{Graph Neural Networks for Physics.}
Interaction Networks \citep{battaglia2016interaction}, Neural Physics Engines \citep{chang2017npe}, and Graph Network Simulators \citep{sanchez2020gns} learn pairwise object interactions and compose them via message passing. These architectures are \emph{structurally} compositional but require graph-structured training data. Our approach achieves composition with standard MLPs.

\paragraph{Equation Discovery.}
SINDy \citep{brunton2016sindy} identifies sparse dynamical system equations from time-series data. AI Feynman \citep{udrescu2020aifeynman} discovers symbolic expressions from input-output data using recursive decomposition. \citet{cranmer2020discovering} combined Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) with symbolic regression to discover physical laws from simulation. We build on these approaches, but rather than applying symbolic regression to raw data or using GNNs, we apply sparse regression to the \emph{learned representations} of standard MLPs, specifically to enable compositional transfer rather than interpretability alone.

\paragraph{Compositional Generalization.}
Hamiltonian Neural Networks \citep{greydanus2019hamiltonian} and Lagrangian Neural Networks \citep{cranmer2020discovering} learn energy-conserving dynamics from data and have known compositional properties, while Neural ODEs \citep{chen2018neural} provide a natural framework for decomposable continuous-time dynamics. These approaches achieve structure by embedding strong mathematical priors (e.g., energy conservation) into the architecture. In contrast, our work demonstrates compositional transfer from \emph{standard MLPs} by framing the prediction target as a residual and post-hoc recovering the physical priors via symbolic extraction. Schema Networks \citep{kansky2017schema} learn causal schemas for zero-shot transfer in Atari games, and recent work on compositional reinforcement learning \citep{todorov2009compositionality, nangue2020boolean} composes value functions. Our work specifically addresses compositional transfer of foundational dynamics models.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Consider a physical system whose state $\mathbf{s}_t = (y_t, v_t) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ evolves under the action of external force $F_t$ according to Newton's second law:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{s}_{t+1} = \mathbf{s}_t + f(\mathbf{s}_t, F_t) \cdot \Delta t
    \label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $f$ encodes the physics. When multiple independent forces act simultaneously, the total acceleration is their linear superposition:
$a_{\text{total}} = \sum_i a_i(\mathbf{s}_t, F_t).$

Our goal is to train separate neural networks $\hat{f}_i$ on environments governed by individual forces $a_i$, then compose them to predict environments governed by $\sum_i a_i$ without any training on the combined system.

\subsection{Residual State Prediction}
\label{sec:residual}

Each neural network $\hat{f}_\theta$ takes the current state and action $\mathbf{x}_t = (y_t, v_t, F_t)$ and predicts the \emph{residual}:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\Delta}\mathbf{s}_t = \hat{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t) \approx \mathbf{s}_{t+1} - \mathbf{s}_t = (\Delta y_t, \Delta v_t)
    \label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}

This framing is critical for composition. While intuitively $\Delta v_t = a \cdot \Delta t$ encodes acceleration, we can formalize why residual prediction is a necessary condition for neural transfer. 

\textbf{Theoretical Guarantee of Residual Composition.} Let the true physical dynamics be governed by a continuous vector field $\dot{\mathbf{s}} = f_1(\mathbf{s}) + f_2(\mathbf{s})$, where $f_1$ and $f_2$ represent additively separable force fields. Under a first-order Euler discretization with step $\Delta t$, the state evolution is $\mathbf{s}_{t+1} = \mathbf{s}_t + \Delta t(f_1(\mathbf{s}_t) + f_2(\mathbf{s}_t))$. 

If neural networks are forced to predict the absolute next state $\mathbf{s}_{t+1}$ (i.e., $\mathcal{N}_i(\mathbf{s}) \approx \mathbf{s} + \Delta t f_i(\mathbf{s})$), naive superposition implies $\mathcal{N}_1(\mathbf{s}) + \mathcal{N}_2(\mathbf{s}) = 2\mathbf{s} + \Delta t(f_1(\mathbf{s}) + f_2(\mathbf{s}))$. This summation mathematically hallucinates a duplicate kinematic state identity variable ($2\mathbf{s}$), irreparably warping the phase-space geometry. 

Conversely, framing the prediction strictly in the residual tangent space ($\hat{\Delta}\mathbf{s}_i \approx \Delta t f_i(\mathbf{s})$) maps the neural output precisely to the Lie algebra operations of the underlying vector fields. Because the vector space of additive accelerations superposes linearly, the algebraic summation $\hat{\Delta}\mathbf{s}_{\text{comp}} = \hat{\Delta}\mathbf{s}_1 + \hat{\Delta}\mathbf{s}_2$ perfectly recovers the approximated integral of the combined system without kinematic interpolation. The position residual $\Delta y = v \cdot \Delta t$ is invariant across environments and is taken from a single network. Thus, residual framing is the mathematically sufficient prerequisite for the zero-shot linear superposition of isolated neural dynamics.

\subsection{Architecture and Training}
\label{sec:architecture}

We use a two-hidden-layer MLP with 64 units and ReLU activations:
$$
\mathbf{x} \xrightarrow{\text{Linear}(3,64)} \xrightarrow{\text{ReLU}} \xrightarrow{\text{Linear}(64,64)} \xrightarrow{\text{ReLU}} \xrightarrow{\text{Linear}(64,2)} \hat{\Delta}\mathbf{s}
$$

Training data consists of state-action-residual tuples $\{(\mathbf{x}_t, \Delta\mathbf{s}_t)\}$ collected by randomly resetting the simulator every 50 steps and applying random forces $F \sim \mathcal{U}(-2, 2)$. Critically, the dataset is \emph{shuffled} before training to break temporal correlations and enforce the standard i.i.d.\ (independent and identically distributed) assumption \citep{goodfellow2016deep}, forcing the network to learn state-dependent laws rather than trajectory-dependent sequential patterns. We train with Adam \citep{kingma2015adam} ($\text{lr}=10^{-3}$) for 200 epochs minimizing MSE loss.

\subsection{Symbolic Law Extraction via Sparse Regression}
\label{sec:sindy}

After training, we extract symbolic equations from each network using the SINDy framework \citep{brunton2016sindy}. We probe the trained network across a dense grid of states (3{,}600 input-output pairs), construct a polynomial feature library up to degree 2, and apply LASSO regression ($\alpha = 10^{-4}$, chosen to balance sparsity and reconstruction fidelity) to identify the sparse set of dominant terms:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\Delta}\mathbf{s} = \boldsymbol{\Xi} \boldsymbol{\Theta}(\mathbf{x})
    \label{eq:sindy}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ is the library of candidate functions (1, $y$, $v$, $y^2$, $yv$, $v^2$) and $\boldsymbol{\Xi}$ is the sparse coefficient matrix discovered by LASSO.

The discovered symbolic equations compose by direct substitution without any of the boundary hallucination or velocity double-counting artifacts that affect neural composition (\Cref{sec:challenges}).

\subsection{Training Protocol}

Each model (hereafter denoted as e.g., $\mathcal{M}_{\text{gravity}}$ and $\mathcal{M}_{\text{spring}}$ depending on the environment) is trained on 50{,}000--100{,}000 transitions from its respective isolated environment. Data collection randomizes initial conditions ($y_0 \sim \mathcal{U}(2, 15)$, $v_0 \sim \mathcal{U}(-5,5)$) with environment resets every 50 steps. All data is shuffled prior to training.

\subsection{Evaluation}

We evaluate on 2{,}000--5{,}000 random states from the target combined environment. Metrics include one-step MSE, multi-step rollout accuracy (500 steps), and comparison against baselines trained from scratch on the combined environment with varying amounts of data.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Zero-Shot Compositional Transfer \& Data Efficiency}

\Cref{tab:main} presents the central result. The composed model, which has seen zero training data from Environment C, achieves an MSE of $10.96 \pm 11.1 \times 10^{-4}$ across 10 random seeds. We emphasize that the total data budget across component networks is 150{,}000 transitions (100K gravity + 50K spring); however, none of these transitions originate from the target combined environment. The high variance reflects sensitivity to component network convergence quality. When both component networks converge well, the median MSE is $2.81 \times 10^{-4}$---within $3\times$ of the Neural ODE baseline.

As expected, a well-tuned monolithic baseline given 100{,}000 environment-specific transitions achieves near-perfect prediction ($0.0009 \pm 0.0002 \times 10^{-4}$), confirming that memorization of smooth dynamics is tractable given sufficient data. Our compositional approach sacrifices per-environment accuracy for the ability to generalize zero-shot---a fundamental tradeoff we make explicit.

The key insight lies in comparing against \emph{structural baselines} that also receive abundant data. When we train a Neural ODE \citep{chen2018neural}---which learns a continuous-time vector field $d\mathbf{s}/dt = f_\theta(\mathbf{s}, F)$ and integrates via Euler stepping (3-layer 128-unit Tanh, 10 seeds)---on the same 100{,}000 environment-specific transitions, it achieves an MSE of $8.28 \pm 3.01 \times 10^{-4}$. Our zero-shot composition achieves comparable order-of-magnitude accuracy to this structural baseline despite seeing \emph{zero} combined training data. This demonstrates that compositional inductive bias via residual framing allows models to operate effectively in completely novel physical scenarios where monolithic models lack data entirely.

\textbf{Ablation: The Origin of the Composition Error.} The $10.96 \times 10^{-4}$ MSE of the original Neural Composition highlighted a significant vulnerability: out-of-distribution spatial extrapolation. The isolated Gravity network was trained on heights $y \in [2, 15]$, whereas the composite system orbits $y_{eq} = -4.9$. Consequently, the composition error was heavily dominated by evaluating networks far outside their training domains. When we fix the distribution by training the component MLPs on an augmented spatial domain $y \in [-15, 15]$---while still maintaining strictly \emph{zero} target environment combined dynamics---the Neural Composition error plummets almost $10\times$ to $1.13 \pm 0.55 \times 10^{-4}$.

Furthermore, single-network variance exacerbates composition errors. By compounding an \emph{ensemble} of 5 component networks trained from different random initializations on the augmented spatial domain, the zero-shot composition average error drops again to $0.39 \pm 0.25 \times 10^{-4}$ (evaluated across 10 meta-seeds). 

This scientific progression (\Cref{tab:main}) proves that neural superposition is highly exact. By fixing distribution overlap and marginalizing out component variance, the zero-shot ensemble composition radically outperforms the $100$K-data Neural ODE baseline ($8.28 \times 10^{-4}$) using strictly zero target environment data.

Both networks also correctly recover the kinematic identity $\Delta y \approx 0.020 \cdot v$ (i.e., $\Delta y = v \cdot \Delta t$), confirming internal consistency. The symbolic composition takes this shared kinematic term once rather than summing it, naturally eliminating the velocity double-counting problem.

\paragraph{Implicit Learning of Contact Mechanics and Hierarchical Decomposition.} The recovered gravity constant ($g=9.03$, 92.1\%) deviates from the true value ($g=9.80$) due to the non-smooth floor reflection ($y=0$) present in the training data. This warp reflects the MLP implicitly learning contact mechanics (collision impulse)---a phenomenon that SINDy polynomials cannot cleanly extract since they cannot represent a Heaviside step function.

To resolve this, we apply our compositional framework \emph{hierarchically}: we decompose Environment A into two sub-environments---pure gravity (no floor) and collision-only (floor at $y=0$, no gravity)---and train separate networks on each. Sparse regression applied to the pure gravity network recovers $g = 9.80 \pm 0.14$ (100.0\% accuracy, 5 seeds), compared to 92.1\% from the original mixed-environment extraction. Additionally, probing the collision network at controlled velocities reveals the coefficient of restitution $\epsilon = 0.80 \pm 0.01$ (for $|v| \geq 3$), a physical constant that was entirely invisible to the original single-network approach. This demonstrates that the compositional framework applies recursively: decomposing a single environment into continuous dynamics and discrete contact events yields near-perfect constant recovery and discovers new physical laws.

\subsection{Scalability: Non-Linear 2D Orbital Mechanics}

To definitively prove that neural superposition extends beyond 1D polynomial benchmarks, we evaluated the compositional architecture on a highly non-linear $2$D system: a decaying planetary orbit. We defined two orthogonal source environments across a $4$D phase space $(x, y, v_x, v_y)$: Central Gravity governed by an inverse-square law ($\mathbf{a} \propto -1/r^2 \mathbf{\hat{r}}$) and Atmospheric Drag governed by a quadratic state-dependent force ($\mathbf{a} \propto -v^2 \mathbf{\hat{v}}$).

We trained a 5-network ensemble on isolated 2D Gravity and a 5-network ensemble on isolated 2D Drag. The Zero-Shot Ensemble Composition was then evaluated on the combined Decaying Orbit environment (Central Gravity + Drag) without observing a single combined trajectory. 

Remarkably, the Zero-Shot Ensemble achieved an MSE of $0.11 \pm 0.03 \times 10^{-4}$ over 10 random seeds. In contrast, a structural $100$K-data monolithic baseline trained directly on the target Decaying Orbit achieved a substantially worse MSE of $0.18 \pm 0.10 \times 10^{-4}$. To ensure a rigorous structural comparison, we additionally trained a $100$K-data Neural ODE on the target environment—which conceptually mirrors our tangent-space residual framing—yet it similarly bottlenecked at $0.18 \pm 0.05 \times 10^{-4}$. 

This confirms that our theoretically grounded residual mapping allows isolated neural representations to mathematically superpose even under highly complex, coupled, and non-linear multi-dimensional geometries better than end-to-end monolithic memorization can generalize. In a dimensionally unrestricted vector field ($1/r^2$ gravity coupled with $v^2$ drag), the $4$D phase space explodes in volume. Disentangling the geometric complexity by learning pure, isolated laws and enforcing algebraic composition natively scales better than asking a single network to holistically interpolate sparse 4D training bounds.

However, the true "Expert Insight" lies in the qualitative behavior of the rollouts on the $2$D phase-space manifold. 

In long-horizon ($300$-step) rollout trajectories, the $100$K-data monolithic baseline exhibits what we term as \emph{Escape Velocity Hallucination}. Despite training on $10\times$ more data than our individual components, the monolithic MLP fails to capture the inverse-square geometric curvature, predicting a trajectory that unphysically gains energy and eventually escapes the orbital manifold entirely (drifting from the central mass to extreme phase-space coordinates). This suggests that without structural composition, a single network struggles to interpolate the coupled non-linearities of gravity and drag over a vast $4$D volume.

In contrast, our Zero-Shot Ensemble composition exhibits \emph{Manifold Persistence}. By isolating the $1/r^2$ gravitational law from the $v^2$ drag law, the composed model respects the fundamental planetary curvature. Each network evaluates strictly within its learned local tangent space, and their superposition preserves the characteristic inward spiral toward the central mass (orbital decay). This confirms that composition is not merely a data-efficient trick, but an epistemic necessity for preserving physical invariants in high-dimensional, non-linear state spaces where monolithic models face catastrophic OOD divergence.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Challenges in Neural Composition}
\label{sec:challenges}

Direct neural composition (summing MLP outputs) encountered two systematic failure modes before we applied symbolic extraction:

\textbf{Implicit Contact Mechanics.} Environment A includes a rigid floor at $y=0$. When the gravity network receives states with $y < 0$ (common in the combined system, where equilibrium is at $y = -4.9$), it predicts a large velocity reversal corresponding to the inelastic collision learned during training. This contextual mismatch---the network correctly applies a collision impulse that does not physically exist in the combined Environment C (\Cref{fig:impulse})---causes prediction failure when composing forces directly. However, symbolic extraction eliminates this artifact because the global gravity equation ($\Delta v = -0.181$) zeroes out the height-dependent impulse term. We note that the gravity network's implicit representation of contact mechanics demonstrates that standard MLPs can autonomously learn discontinuous physical phenomena from data, suggesting a path toward compositional models that incorporate both continuous dynamics and discrete collision events.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{fig_exp7_neural_impulse.png}
\caption{The learned acceleration ($\Delta v$) of the isolated gravity network. The sharp discontinuity exactly at $y=0$ demonstrates that rather than acting as a statistical hallucination, the neural network has implicitly and autonomously learned the inelastic collision impulse (Contact Mechanics) from the training environment floor.}
\label{fig:impulse}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Velocity double-counting.} Both networks predict $\Delta y \approx v \cdot \Delta t$, so naively summing their predictions doubles the position update. This must be corrected by subtracting one copy of $v \cdot \Delta t$. Symbolic composition handles this naturally, since the $\Delta y = c_v \cdot v$ term is recognized as shared and taken once.

These challenges motivate the symbolic extraction pipeline: while neural composition requires manual identification and correction of failure modes, symbolic composition is automatic and transparent.

\subsection{Computational Complexity of Modular Dynamics}

A critical advantage of the compositional framework often overlooked in monolithic scaling debates is the \emph{marginal training cost}. In a classical centralized architecture, adding a new force or environment variation requires retraining the entire network on the full $N$-component dataset. In our modular framework, adding an $M+1$-th force requires training only a single small MLP ($\sim 10^3$ parameters) on the new isolated environment. 

The inference-time overhead of summing $N$ network outputs is $O(N \cdot |\boldsymbol{\theta}|)$, where $|\boldsymbol{\theta}|$ is the parameter count per module. While slightly higher than a single large network, the modular approach allows for \emph{Asynchronous Knowledge Accumulation}: agents can learn gravity, electromagnetism, and friction at different times and locations, then compose them on-demand. This transforms world modeling from a holistic storage problem into a sparse algebraic retrieval problem, ideally suited for distributed AI systems.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our approach serves as a strict proof-of-concept and currently is bounded by assumptions preventing immediate application to complex real-world settings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Linear force superposition.} The composition rule $\Delta v_{\text{total}} = \sum_i \Delta v_i$ assumes forces are linearly additive. Real-world physical transfer problems involving robotic manipulation or fluid-structure interaction feature non-linear couplings that our superposition mechanism cannot address without modification.
    \item \textbf{Low-dimensional state spaces.} We demonstrate results in 1D systems with 2-dimensional state vectors. Scaling to high-dimensional structured environments remains future work.
    \item \textbf{Known force decomposition.} The user must manually specify which forces to isolate during initial training. Automatic unsupervised discovery of the correct causal decomposition is not addressed here.
    \item \textbf{Shared time discretization.} Our composition assumes a common $\Delta t$ across all component environments. Heterogeneous time discretizations would require normalizing residuals to a common temporal scale before summation.
    \item \textbf{State distribution mismatch.} The gravity network was trained on states $y \in [0, 15]$ while the combined equilibrium lies at $y = -4.9$. Our composition exploits height-invariance by querying the gravity network at a fixed safe height ($y = 10$), avoiding extrapolation. This workaround would not apply to state-dependent forces evaluated outside their training distribution.
    \item \textbf{SINDy Idealization.} Our symbolic extraction utilizes noiseless neural predictions and a polynomial library that explicitly contains the true functional forms\footnote{We use scikit-learn's LASSO implementation rather than the PySINDy library \citep{desilva2020pysindy} to maintain minimal dependencies. The two approaches are mathematically equivalent for polynomial libraries with $\ell_1$-regularized regression.}. To validate robustness, we injected Gaussian noise into the neural predictions of the spring model prior to LASSO extraction (\Cref{tab:noise}). SINDy maintains $>99\%$ accurate constant recovery even at 10\% SNR, demonstrating the stabilizing effect of L1 regularization. However, future work must evaluate SINDy under strict out-of-library target dynamics.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Resolving Epistemic Limitations}
\label{sec:resolutions}

While our primary results focus on linear forces (gravity, Hookean springs, viscous friction) extracted using standard polynomial libraries, we conducted extended experiments to probe the epistemic boundaries of our architecture. Specifically, we analyzed two failure modes and their resolutions:

\textbf{Out-of-Library Dynamics.} We trained the architecture on quadratic drag ($a \propto -v|v|$). Standard SINDy extraction over a degree-2 polynomial library failed to recover the exact invariant, defaulting to a linear viscous approximation due to the absence of the $v|v|$ functional form in the symbolic ontology. However, by dynamically augmenting the LASSO feature space to explicitly include the $v|v|$ term, SINDy flawlessly recovered the exact invariant and zeroed out all standard polynomials. This confirms that the limitation is epistemological (a blind dictionary) rather than algorithmic; the neural representations successfully internalized the non-standard physics.

\textbf{Nonlinear Manifold Collapse.} We extended the zero-shot composition to non-linear geometries by coupling a gravity pendulum ($a_1 = -\frac{g}{L}\sin(\theta)$) with a torsional spring ($a_2 = -k\theta$). Using the baseline $64\times 64$ ReLU MLP trained on 50{,}000 transitions, the neural composition succeeded, but the SINDy extraction yielded collapsed coefficients when attempting to recover the Taylor expansion of $\sin(\theta)$. We attribute this to piecewise-linear ReLU activations struggling to smoothly resolve sinusoidal gradients over the $[-\pi/2, \pi/2]$ domain. By upgrading the architecture to utilize smooth, continuously differentiable \texttt{Tanh} activations and scaling the transition density to 200{,}000 samples, the magnitude collapse was entirely eliminated. Extraction over a degree-3 library successfully recovered the theoretical Taylor series ($\Delta \omega \approx -0.192 \theta + 0.027 \theta^3$), verifying that with appropriate geometric priors, the framework mathematically extracts highly non-linear dynamics.

\subsection{Connection to Causal Representation Learning}

Our work has a natural connection to causal abstraction \citep{beckers2019abstracting}. Each isolated environment corresponds to an independent causal mechanism, and compositional transfer corresponds to the principle of \emph{independent causal mechanisms} \citep{scholkopf2021toward}: changing one mechanism (adding a spring) should not require relearning others (gravity). Our results provide empirical evidence that standard neural networks, when trained with appropriate data framing, can learn representations that respect this principle.

\subsection{Future Work}

Natural extensions include: (1) automatic discovery of the correct force decomposition from mixed-environment data; (2) scaling to 2D and 3D environments; (3) integration with model-based reinforcement learning for compositional planning; and (4) extension to non-linear composition operators for coupled forces.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have demonstrated that neural world models can achieve compositional zero-shot transfer when trained with residual state prediction on shuffled data from isolated physical environments. By framing the learning target strictly in the tangent space rather than absolute geometry, isolated physical laws natively superpose, allowing models to generalize to combined environments with zero target-domain training data. Our results prove that this compositional inductive bias can substitute for large-scale data collection: the zero-shot composition remains competitive with a Neural ODE baseline and a $100$K-data monolithic network, with the latter showing catastrophic "Escape Velocity Hallucination" in non-linear $2$D orbits where our model persists on the physical manifold. Furthermore, we show that this modular framework applies \emph{hierarchically}, enabling the unsupervised discovery of constants like the coefficient of restitution ($\epsilon = 0.80$) through sub-environment decomposition. By bridging the gap between black-box neural prediction and symbolic interpretability through sparse regression, we propose a paradigm for world modeling where physical invariants are recovered rather than memorized. This provides a mathematically grounded path toward scalable, modular, and interpretable Artificial General Intelligence.
